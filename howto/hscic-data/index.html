Finding data at HSCIC

The Health and Social Care Information Centre has a tremendous collection of open data that allows us to track how our hospitals are doing, see how our GP Surgeries are performing and see how drugs are being prescribed across England.

Tutorial

Finding data at HSCIC

1

Tip One - Searching for Data at HSCIC

It all starts with the search. We'll highlight what we feel are some of the gems in the HSCIC data sets, but if you don't find what you are after here then you can see what you can find with HSCIC's Search Catalogue.

You can also engage with the people behind the Open Data at the Information Centre. They run a number of very responsive and friendly twitter accounts @HSCIC and @HSCICOpenData.

Often when I'm looking for the best data to answer my question a friendly tweet to them will point me to some data that I didn't know existed or data that is more up to date than the one I knew about.

The twitter accounts are also great places to ask questions about the data sets you find and discuss potential problems or errors you might see in the data.

2

Tip Two - Indicators Portal

The Indicators Portal is probably your next port of call as you are searching for data.

Here you'll find lots of Excel spreadsheets that can be turned into csv files that you can process with other tools. Most of the data isn't what you would call big, but there is a wide variety of it.

There is data on Clinical Commissioning Groups (CCGs), GP Practices, Adult Social Care, Hospital Mortality and the NHS Outcomes Framework.

The Indicators Portal is where we were able to find the Diabetes Prevalence data that we used to create the maps on the Open Health Data Platform.

Tip Three - GP Practice Indicators

Hidden deep in the guts of the Indicator Portal is one of the best files for data analysis on the Indicators Portal. The GP Practice Indicators which can be found in a complete .zip file.

As you can see on the left each line has a practice code and name and which CCG that practice belongs to.

There are also indicators on demography, infrastructure, patient experience and other patient care indicators. It feels a bit like Trip Advisor for GP Surgeries.

4

Tip Four - Organisational Data

While the GP Practice Indicators can give you the organisational relationships between the GP's Surgeries and their parent Clinical Commissioning Groups.

You need to download the clearly named Epraccur.csv to get all the addresses so you can put them on a map or do other geographical analyses.

Tip Five - Prescriptions Data

The Prescribing by GP Practice data set is perhaps one of the best data exhaust sets at HSCIC.

The data itself is produced by the NHS Business Services Authority and is used to pay GPs based on how many and what kind of drugs they've prescribed. We can use it with other information from things like the British National Formulary or NICE Guidelines to use it to get a rough estimate of the kinds of health issues in an area or see how prescriptions vary regionally. Combining this with the prevalence data in some of the indicators files can also give you an idea about how much is being spent per person who has a condition as well.

5

Tip Five - Geographic Data

Finding geographic data can actually be quite tricky. Clinical Commissioning Groups are based on Census Regions like Output Areas and Super Output Areas, but finding a good source to use for mapping with something like Spruce Leaf can be tough.

We've solved some of that problem for you by having a Topojson file here. Remember though that the geographical information is based on Ordnance Survey data and their license requires that you cite them when you use it.
